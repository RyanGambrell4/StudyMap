How to interpret these numbers

The averages shown here are averages of accepted students — not minimum cutoffs. A 3.73 average at MD programs means that half of accepted students had higher GPAs and half had lower. Being at or above the average improves your chances but doesn't guarantee admission; being below the average doesn't rule you out.

GPA vs. other factors by program type

Medical school: GPA and MCAT are the two most screened variables at the initial application stage. A GPA below 3.0 essentially eliminates most MD programs. Below 3.5 significantly narrows your viable list. The science GPA (BCPM: Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Math) is tracked separately and given extra weight.

Law school: LSAT score can compensate for a lower GPA more than in medicine. Many law schools explicitly report LSAT/GPA grids showing the range of outcomes. If your GPA is below a school's median, a strong LSAT score becomes more important.

MBA: Work experience, essays, and leadership matter as much or more than GPA in MBA admissions. Schools like Harvard Business School and INSEAD routinely admit candidates with 3.0-3.3 GPAs who have exceptional career trajectories. GPA is a data point, not a gate.

PhD: Research fit with a specific faculty member is often the deciding factor. A strong GRE and research experience can overcome a 3.5 GPA. Funding decisions also drive admits — some departments fund heavily and accept selectively; others accept more broadly with less funding.
