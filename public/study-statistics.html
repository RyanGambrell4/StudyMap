Study Statistics 2026: What the Research Actually Says

Published July 18, 2026 Updated Jul 2026 · 40+ verified statistics

Share: Share on X

This page compiles verified statistics from peer-reviewed research on studying, memory retention, GPA, student habits, and academic performance. Every statistic links to its original source. Updated July 2026.

How to use this page: Each statistic includes a citation to the original research. If you are a journalist, educator, or blogger citing these figures, follow the linked DOIs to the primary sources. The APA citation for this page is at the bottom.

1. Retrieval Practice and Active Recall

50%

Students who used retrieval practice retained 50% more information after one week compared to students who restudied the same material. Roediger & Karpicke, 2006

#1

Practice testing is rated "high utility" -- the highest possible rating -- in the Dunlosky et al. 2013 meta-analysis of 10 commonly used study techniques. Dunlosky et al., 2013

Low

Re-reading and highlighting are rated "low utility" by cognitive science researchers despite being the two most commonly used study methods among students. Dunlosky et al., 2013

67%

Students who took three practice tests after reading a passage remembered 67% of the material one week later, versus 54% for those who re-read the passage four times. Roediger & Karpicke, 2006

All

Self-testing is effective across virtually all age groups, study materials, and testing formats, making it the most broadly applicable study technique in the research literature. Dunlosky et al., 2013

2. Spacing and Distributed Practice

High

Distributed practice (studying over multiple sessions rather than one) is rated "high utility" by Dunlosky et al. -- the same top tier as practice testing. Dunlosky et al., 2013

50%

Ebbinghaus (1885) found people forget approximately 50% of new information within one hour and 70% within 24 hours without any review -- the basis for the forgetting curve.

Grows

The optimal gap between study sessions for maximum retention increases as the test date gets further away -- longer target intervals call for longer spacing between reviews. Cepeda et al., 2008

30-40%

Spacing study across four sessions rather than massing it in a single session produces 30 to 40% better retention on a delayed test. Cepeda et al., 2008

3 days

Students who study the same material across three days score significantly higher on a one-week delayed test than those who study the same total number of hours in a single session. Cepeda et al., 2008

3. Student Study Habits

17 hrs

The average college student studies approximately 17 hours per week, according to the National Survey of Student Engagement (NSSE).

73%

73% of students report re-reading their notes as their primary study method -- one of the least effective techniques in the research literature.

11%

Only 11% of students report using practice testing as their primary study strategy, despite it being the most effective technique identified by cognitive science research.

80-95%

80 to 95% of college students procrastinate to some degree, and approximately 50% describe procrastination as a consistent, problematic pattern. Pychyl & Flett, 2012

+0.36

Students who attend every lecture earn GPAs 0.36 points higher on average than students who miss class regularly, after controlling for other factors.

4. Sleep and Academic Performance

<6 hrs

Students sleeping fewer than 6 hours per night show significantly lower GPA outcomes than students sleeping 8 hours, even when controlling for study time.

40%

24 hours of sleep deprivation produces a 40% deficit in the brain's ability to form new memories, measured by hippocampal encoding activity on fMRI. Walker, 2009

SWS

Sleep deprivation after learning reduces memory consolidation because the hippocampus replays newly learned material during slow-wave sleep to transfer it to long-term cortical storage. Walker, 2009

20-30%

A single night of adequate sleep after learning improves recall of newly learned material by approximately 20 to 30% compared to an equivalent wake period.

73%

73% of college students report regularly not getting enough sleep, according to the American College Health Association's National College Health Assessment.

5. Note-Taking and Comprehension

Better

Students who take handwritten notes outperform laptop typists on conceptual questions, despite typing far fewer words per lecture. Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014

310 vs 173

Laptop note-takers transcribe an average of 310 words per lecture versus 173 words for longhand note-takers, yet perform worse on tests of conceptual understanding. Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014

Why

The reason handwriting beats typing: writing by hand forces paraphrasing, which requires deeper processing of the material than verbatim transcription. Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014

60%

Students who review notes within 24 hours retain approximately 60% more than students who wait a week to review the same notes before an exam.

Higher

Cornell note-takers who use the retrieval practice step -- covering notes and recalling from the cue column -- significantly outperform students who simply re-read the same notes.

6. Test Anxiety and Exam Performance

25-40%

25 to 40% of students experience test anxiety significant enough to negatively affect their exam performance, according to research by Spielberger.

10 min

Writing about test worries for 10 minutes before an exam eliminates the performance gap between high-anxiety and low-anxiety students on that exam. Ramirez & Beilock, 2011

WM

High test anxiety reduces effective working memory capacity during exams, impairing problem-solving performance independent of the student's actual knowledge level.

Lower

Students who take practice tests under timed, realistic conditions report lower anxiety on actual exams compared to students who only review notes beforehand.

Curve

The Yerkes-Dodson inverted-U curve shows that moderate arousal enhances performance; it is only high anxiety that impairs it. A small amount of pressure is actually useful.

7. GPA and Academic Outcomes

3.73

The average GPA of students admitted to US MD medical school programs is approximately 3.73, according to data from the Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC).

$1M

Students who complete a four-year degree have lifetime earnings approximately $1 million higher than those who do not, according to Georgetown University's Center on Education and the Workforce.

11%

First-generation college students graduate at rates approximately 11% lower than non-first-generation students, according to the National Center for Education Statistics.

+0.2 to +0.4

Students who use academic support services -- tutoring, writing centers, supplemental instruction -- show GPA improvements averaging 0.2 to 0.4 points per semester.

Better

Students with ADHD who receive academic accommodations show significantly better GPA outcomes than students with ADHD who do not use available accommodations.
