Why students quit Anki

Anki's algorithm is brilliant. But most students abandon it within a month — not because it doesn't work, but because staying consistent with it is a full-time job in itself.

✍️

Every card is manual

Writing good Anki cards takes real skill and time. Most students spend more time building decks than actually reviewing them.

📚

No study schedule

Anki tells you which cards to review today — not when to study, how long to spend per course, or how to prioritize around exams.

🗂️

Deck management overhead

Organizing decks, sub-decks, tags, and card templates eats into the time you should be spending studying.

📵

Mobile costs $25

Anki's desktop app is free. But the iOS app costs $25 — and studying on your phone requires it.

🧩

Flashcards only

Anki does one thing: spaced repetition flashcards. No quizzes, no session structure, no AI coaching, no grade tracking.

📉

No progress visibility

Anki shows card retention statistics — but not whether your grades are actually improving or which courses need more time.

When Anki is still the right choice

Anki is genuinely excellent — in specific situations. If you're a medical student with thousands of high-yield facts to memorize over years, Anki's algorithm and community decks (Anking, for example) are unmatched. If you're learning a language and need daily vocabulary drilling, Anki's mature ecosystem is hard to beat.

For most students — especially those managing multiple courses, tracking grades, and needing a complete study system — the manual overhead of Anki is a net negative. StudyEdge AI gives you spaced repetition inside a system that does everything else too.
