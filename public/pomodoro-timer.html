How the Pomodoro Technique works

The technique was developed by Francesco Cirillo in the late 1980s while he was a university student. He used a tomato-shaped kitchen timer (pomodoro is Italian for tomato) to work in 25-minute blocks, which helped him push through tasks that felt overwhelming.

The core loop is simple: pick one task, work on it for 25 minutes without switching, take a 5-minute break, then repeat. Every four sessions, take a longer 20-minute break. The constraint matters — knowing the session ends in 25 minutes makes it easier to start.

Why it works for studying

Most studying fails because of context-switching and vague session boundaries. Pomodoros solve both: you work on one thing, and you know exactly when it ends. Research on attention restoration suggests short breaks genuinely recover cognitive resources rather than just being rest.
