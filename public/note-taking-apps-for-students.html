Note-Taking Apps

Best note-taking apps for college students in 2026.

OneNote, Notion, GoodNotes, Obsidian, and Apple Notes, compared honestly for college use. Plus the piece every note-taking app misses: turning your notes into actual study sessions.

What makes a note-taking app actually useful for students

The best note-taking app for college is the one you will actually open when class starts, search through when studying, and not spend more time organizing than using. Here are the criteria that matter in practice:

  • Cross-platform access. Your notes need to be on the device you use in class and on the device you use to study. An app that is only on your iPad is a problem if your study desk has a Windows laptop.
  • Search that works. You will take hundreds of pages of notes per semester. Search is how you find things fast. Test the search before committing to any app.
  • Low friction to start. The best note-taking system is the one you actually use. An app that requires 45 minutes of template setup before you can write your first note will be abandoned in week two.
  • Typing and handwriting support. STEM courses often need handwritten diagrams and equations. Humanities courses are mostly typed. Your app should handle both, or you need two separate apps with a clear workflow between them.

The 5 main options, ranked honestly

1. Microsoft OneNote — Best overall

Free. Available on Windows, Mac, iOS, Android, and web.

OneNote is the most practical choice for most college students. It organizes notes into Notebooks (one per course), Sections (one per unit or topic), and Pages (individual class sessions or topics). This hierarchy maps naturally to how courses are structured.

OneNote handles both typed and handwritten notes. On an iPad with an Apple Pencil, it works as well as any dedicated handwriting app. The search function is strong: it can find text inside handwritten notes. It is entirely free and integrates with Microsoft 365, which most universities provide at no cost.

Best for: Most students. Surface/iPad users who mix typing and handwriting. Students who need a reliable, zero-setup option.

Weakness: The interface is older and less visually polished than Notion. Advanced organization (linking between notes, databases) requires workarounds.

2. GoodNotes — Best for iPad handwriting

Paid ($9.99 one-time or $1.99/month). iOS and iPadOS only.

GoodNotes is the gold standard for handwritten notes on iPad. The handwriting experience is smoother than OneNote, the PDF annotation tools are excellent, and the notebook organization is intuitive. STEM students who work through a lot of problems by hand often prefer GoodNotes over everything else.

The limitation is that GoodNotes is Apple-only. If you switch to a Windows machine to study, you cannot access your notes without a workaround (exporting to PDF). For students whose entire workflow is on Apple devices, this is not a problem.

Best for: iPad users who primarily handwrite. STEM students who work through problem sets by hand.

Weakness: Apple ecosystem only. No web access. Handwriting-to-text search works but imperfectly.

3. Notion — Best for systems thinkers

Free for students. Available on all platforms.

Notion is extremely powerful and extremely flexible. You can build a full semester dashboard with linked databases, task trackers, reading lists, and study schedules, all inside one workspace. Students who build a good Notion system find it genuinely valuable.

The problem is the setup cost. Notion does not give you a structure; you have to build one. First-year students who spend their first weekend building their "perfect" Notion system often spend more time adjusting the system than using it. The flexibility is what makes Notion great and what makes it a time sink for students who should be studying instead of designing templates.

Best for: Students who enjoy building productivity systems. Later-year students who know their workflow. Anyone who needs a combined notes-and-project-management tool.

Weakness: High setup cost. Slow on large databases. Easy to over-engineer instead of studying.

4. Obsidian — Best for connecting ideas

Free for personal use. Available on all platforms.

Obsidian stores notes as plain-text Markdown files on your device. The killer feature is bidirectional linking: you can link between notes so that a concept you study in one course can connect to the same concept appearing in another course. For students who do a lot of interdisciplinary work or who want to build a knowledge base that grows across semesters, Obsidian is uniquely powerful.

Like Notion, Obsidian has a learning curve. The plugin ecosystem is extensive, which means there is a customization rabbit hole waiting for anyone who goes looking for it. It also requires more setup than OneNote before it is useful.

Best for: Students who want to build a long-term knowledge base. Philosophy, law, and humanities students who write a lot. Technical students who enjoy working with plain-text tools.

Weakness: Steep learning curve. Mobile sync requires a paid plan or workaround. No built-in handwriting support.

5. Apple Notes — Best for simplicity

Free. Apple ecosystem only.

Apple Notes is underrated. It is instant to open, search is fast, it syncs reliably across Apple devices, and the interface gets out of your way. Recent updates added tags, folders, and basic linking. For students who want to take notes without thinking about the app, Apple Notes is a legitimate option, as long as they are in the Apple ecosystem.

Best for: Students who want zero friction and are fully on Apple devices. Students who keep notes simple and search when they need something.

Weakness: Apple-only. No Windows or Android access. Limited organization compared to OneNote or Notion.

The piece every note-taking app misses

Here is the uncomfortable truth about note-taking apps: taking good notes does not make you study effectively. The research on learning is clear, passive review of notes is one of the least effective study methods available. What actually builds durable memory is active retrieval: testing yourself on the material rather than reading through it again.

Every note-taking app gives you a place to store information. None of them help you turn that information into flashcards, generate practice questions, or build a study schedule that distributes review over time.

FAQ

Common questions about note-taking apps.

What is the best note-taking app for college students?

Microsoft OneNote is the best overall choice for most college students: free, cross-platform, handles typing and handwriting, and organizes naturally by course. iPad users who primarily handwrite should look at GoodNotes. Students who want to build a connected knowledge base may prefer Obsidian.

Is Notion good for college notes?

Notion is powerful but has a significant setup cost. Students who invest in building a good Notion workspace find it extremely useful. Students who do not have that time up front often find it easier to start with OneNote and switch to Notion once they know what system they actually need.

Is GoodNotes worth it for college?

GoodNotes is worth it if you have an iPad and do a lot of handwriting, especially for STEM courses with equations, diagrams, and problem sets. For laptop-only students or those in primarily text-based courses, the advantage over OneNote's handwriting mode is smaller.
