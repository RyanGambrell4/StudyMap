How AP exams are scored

AP exams have two sections that are weighted together into a composite score, which is then converted to a 1-5 scale. The exact conversion depends on overall student performance and changes slightly each year — which is why College Board doesn't publish fixed cutoffs in advance.

Multiple choice section

AP multiple choice questions are scored with no penalty for wrong answers. Your raw MC score is simply the number of questions you answered correctly. Each question is worth one raw point.

Free response section

Free response sections vary significantly by exam. AP Calculus AB has 6 FRQ problems worth a total of 54 points. AP English Language has 3 essays scored by trained readers on a 1-9 scale each. APUSH has a DBQ and two LEQs with their own rubrics. This calculator uses simplified estimates based on published scoring guidelines.

The composite score

The College Board weights each section (typically 50/50) and adds them together. The resulting composite is then mapped to a 1-5 score based on that year's score cutoffs. Higher-performing years tend to have higher cutoffs for a 5; lower-performing cohorts may see the cutoff drop. Year-to-year variation is usually small (1-3 composite points per score boundary).
