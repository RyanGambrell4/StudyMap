When Notion works, and when you need more

Notion is genuinely useful as a productivity tool. It is not a study tool, and pretending it is causes most students to abandon their workspace before midterms.

Notion is great for raw notes

Lectures, project planning, syllabus tracking, club work, personal docs. Notion's flexibility shines when you need to design your own structure. Keep using it for that.

Notion is not great for actually studying

A to-do list with class names is not a study plan. Saving a vocab list is not flashcards. Watching the page sit there does not produce retrieval practice. The study work needs different tools.

Templates decay

Every Notion student template starts pristine and ends with three half-filled databases and a course page that has not been touched in a month. This is universal. The tool fights you when you want to use it the same way every week.

StudyEdge AI does the study work

Schedule generation, AI flashcards, an AI tutor, practice quizzes, active recall, grade tracking. None of which Notion does, and all of which you would have to build yourself there.

Use both, smarter

Most students who try StudyEdge AI keep Notion. Notion for class notes and personal docs. StudyEdge AI for the actual study workflow. They do not compete. They sit next to each other.

Built around your real semester

StudyEdge AI knows your exam dates, your courses, your grade targets, and what you have struggled with. It uses all of it to plan every session. A Notion page sits there with the information; StudyEdge AI does something with it.
