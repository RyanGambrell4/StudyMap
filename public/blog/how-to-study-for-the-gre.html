How to Study for GRE

The GRE is the SAT for graduate school: verbal, quant, and a writing section, with a vocabulary list that makes most takers feel underprepared. Here is how to prepare in 2 to 6 months, depending on your target score and starting point.

Key Takeaways
  • GRE Verbal tests vocabulary in context and analytical reading, not definitions -- reading academic writing regularly is the most efficient long-term prep
  • Quant covers algebra, geometry, arithmetic, and data interpretation at approximately 10th-grade level -- content is basic, traps are subtle
  • The 170-question GRE vocabulary list is manageable; learning these root-word families (benevolent/malevolent, loquacious/reticent) teaches patterns, not just words
  • Analytical Writing tasks are scored on argument structure and evidence quality, not writing elegance -- template your essay structure before exam day
  • Two full-length timed practice tests with official materials are the minimum before your test date; score without stopping to review until the full test is done

What the GRE actually measures

The verbal section is heavily vocabulary-dependent. Two of the three question types (Text Completion, Sentence Equivalence) reward knowing specific GRE-favorite words. Reading Comprehension is about extracting argument structure under time. Quant is roughly SAT-level math reframed: arithmetic, algebra, geometry, data interpretation. Writing is two essays evaluated on argument structure.

Average GRE preparation takes between 1 and 3 months and 100 or more study hours to reach a competitive target score for graduate school admissions. Students who compress preparation into the final 2 weeks consistently underperform relative to those who spread the same hours across 6-8 weeks.

Diagnose first

Take a full timed practice test before doing any prep. The ETS PowerPrep is free and gives you the closest simulation of test day. Whichever section is weakest gets the most prep time. If your verbal raw is significantly lower than your quant, the GRE plan has to be vocabulary-heavy.

Vocabulary, the part most people skip

300 to 1,000 GRE vocabulary words is a realistic prep target. Build the list early and review daily. Magoosh's word list, Manhattan's 500 essentials, or Barron's 1100 are reputable sources. Spaced repetition is the only sustainable method at this volume, an AI flashcard maker can turn any word list into a review deck immediately.

Quant by topic, drilled

Do 15 problems of one type back to back. 15 ratios and proportions. 15 geometry. 15 word problems. The drill installs the pattern. Generic mixed practice teaches you to recognize types you already see; targeted drills add the ones you do not.

Use Powerprep for realism

ETS's PowerPrep is the most realistic practice. Take both free tests, save them for later in your prep when you can use the scores to calibrate. The paid PowerPrep PLUS tests add depth.

Writing: argue, do not just opine

The Analytical Writing section rewards explicit argument structure: thesis, three reasons, examples. Conclusion. Practice writing 5 essays under timed conditions. Most people study 0 writing essays and lose easy points.

Build a 6-week, 3-month, or 6-month plan

A 6-week plan suits returning students with solid foundations who need to refresh and run practice tests. A 3-month plan handles a moderate vocabulary gap or quant rust. A 6-month plan is for students starting near zero or aiming for the 90th percentile. Pick by where you are now, not by what feels ambitious.

The GRE verbal section rewards depth of vocabulary knowledge, not breadth. Students who understand the precise meaning and connotation of 800 high-frequency words will outperform students who have a shallow familiarity with 2,000 words.

The test day plan

Light review only. The morning of, run through a couple of vocab words and a quant problem to warm up. Bring a snack. Use the breaks. Pace yourself: in adaptive sections, missing the first few questions hurts more than missing the last few.

The bottom line on GRE prep

The GRE rewards vocabulary depth and quantitative reasoning speed above most other factors. Students who focus primarily on content review without building in timed section practice tend to underperform on test day because time pressure generates different error patterns than untimed work. Starting timed section practice from week one -- not just in the final weeks before the test -- produces more reliable score gains because it trains both the knowledge and the pacing simultaneously.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should you study for the GRE?

Most students benefit from 2 to 4 months of preparation. Students targeting top-10 PhD or professional programs who are more than 10 percentile points below the program's average may need 4 to 6 months. The GRE rewards both content knowledge and test-taking strategy. A structured plan with full-length practice tests every 2 weeks, combined with targeted section review, produces better results than daily studying without practice exams.

What is a good GRE score for graduate school?

GRE score expectations vary significantly by field. STEM programs often weight the Quantitative score heavily (a 165+ is competitive for top programs). Humanities programs may weight Verbal more. The average Quant score is approximately 153 and the average Verbal score is approximately 151 on the 130 to 170 scale. Research the average scores of admitted students at your target programs rather than using general benchmarks.

How is the GRE structured?

The GRE consists of Verbal Reasoning (two 30-minute sections, approximately 40 questions total), Quantitative Reasoning (two 35-minute sections, approximately 40 questions total), and one Analytical Writing section (60 minutes, one essay). The exam includes one unscored experimental section that does not affect your score. Scores are reported on a 130 to 170 scale for Verbal and Quant, and 0 to 6 for Writing.

How do you improve your GRE Verbal score?

GRE Verbal tests vocabulary in context, reading comprehension, and argument analysis. Building vocabulary through word lists (Barron's 800 Essential GRE Words is a common resource) helps with Text Completion and Sentence Equivalence questions. For Reading Comprehension, practice identifying the main idea, supporting evidence, and author's purpose before looking at questions. For each wrong answer, identify whether the error was vocabulary, comprehension, or inference.

Can you retake the GRE if you get a low score?

Yes. You can take the GRE up to five times per year, with a required 21-day gap between attempts. Most graduate programs accept ScoreSelect, which lets you choose which scores to send. Taking the GRE once with adequate preparation, reviewing your section performance, and targeting specific weak areas before a second attempt is the most effective approach. Retaking without changing your preparation method rarely produces meaningful score improvements.
