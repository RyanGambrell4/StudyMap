MCAT Study Schedule: 6 Months Out

Six months is the window most successful MCAT applicants use. Long enough to do real content review without burning out, short enough that the material stays fresh by test day. Here is a realistic week-by-week plan, structured the way the students who score above 515 actually use the time.

Key Takeaways
  • A 6-month MCAT schedule requires roughly 300-400 total study hours -- averaging 15-20 hours per week with strategic increases in the final month
  • The MCAT tests cross-disciplinary reasoning: a single passage may draw from biology, chemistry, physics, and psychology simultaneously
  • ANKI content review (using AnKing or Zanki decks) should begin in month 1; spaced repetition has time to work across 6 months
  • Full-length MCAT practice tests should begin by month 3 -- your AAMC FL scores at month 3 establish your baseline and show where to invest remaining time
  • CARS (Critical Analysis and Reasoning) requires daily passage practice from week one; you cannot prepare for it in the final weeks

The three phases of a six-month MCAT plan

Almost every effective MCAT plan splits into three phases. Phase one is content review, where you fill the gaps in your science background. Phase two is question practice, where you learn to apply that content under MCAT-style passages. Phase three is full-length tests, where you build stamina and refine timing. Six months gives roughly twelve weeks for phase one, eight weeks for phase two, and four weeks for phase three.

Most competitive MCAT scores require 300-500 hours of preparation, spread across 3-6 months. Students who attempt to compress that preparation into 6-8 weeks -- even with longer daily sessions -- consistently underperform compared to students who spread the same total hours across a full schedule. The bottleneck is consolidation time between sessions, not total hours logged.

Hours per week, realistically

If you are studying alongside classes, plan for 15 to 20 focused hours per week. If you are studying full-time during summer, plan for 35 to 40. More than 50 hours a week consistently is a path to burnout that hurts your score, not a path to a higher one.

Phase 1: Content review (weeks 1 through 12)

Use the official AAMC content outline as your map. Cover roughly:

  • Weeks 1 to 3: General chemistry and biochemistry
  • Weeks 4 to 5: Physics
  • Weeks 6 to 8: Biology
  • Weeks 9 to 10: Psychology and sociology
  • Weeks 11 to 12: Organic chemistry (often the weakest area for non-chem majors)

The exact ordering matters less than the total coverage. Some students prefer to interleave subjects rather than block them. Either works. What does not work is skipping the official content outline because you "remember" the material from class.

The rule for phase one: do questions every week

Even during content review, you should be doing some MCAT-style passages every week from the first week. Do not save questions for later. The MCAT is a reasoning test wearing science content as a costume. The reasoning is what you practice. Phase 2: Question phase (weeks 13 through 20)

Move primary effort from content review to question banks. AAMC official questions are the gold standard. Third-party banks like UWorld are widely used. Daily question sets of 30 to 60, reviewed thoroughly afterward, are the workhorse of this phase. The review is more important than the questions. For every question you get wrong and every question you got right but were uncertain on, write down: what the question was actually testing, what the right reasoning chain was, and what trap you fell into. This document compounds over weeks.

Phase 3: Full-length tests (weeks 21 through 24)

Full-length practice tests are the hardest part of MCAT prep because they take a full day each. Plan one per week for the last four weeks. AAMC full lengths first, since they are the most representative. Take each one under real conditions: same start time as the actual exam, same breaks, no phone. Then spend two full days reviewing every passage. Reviewing a full length takes longer than taking it.

The last week before the MCAT

Do not study new content. Do not take a final full length the day before. Review your error log. Sleep on a normal schedule. Take the morning of the exam seriously: eat something, leave early, do a 20-minute warm review of high-yield areas. Most score variance in the last week comes from sleep and stress, not from cramming.

The biggest mistake MCAT students make in a 6-month schedule is spending the first 4 months entirely on content review. By month 4, the content from month 1 has decayed significantly. Full-length practice exams should begin by month 2 and become the primary activity by month 4.

Common mistakes that wreck a six-month plan

  • Spending too long on content review. The questions teach you the most. Move into them by week 12 even if content review feels incomplete.
  • Ignoring CARS until late. Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills cannot be crammed. Read complex non-science writing daily from week one.
  • Not reviewing wrong answers thoroughly. The review document is where you actually improve.
  • Taking too many full lengths. Five to seven total is usually right. More than that and you fatigue out before the real test.

The bottom line on 6-month MCAT prep

Six months is enough time to produce major score improvements across all four MCAT sections, but only if the schedule includes full-length practice exams from month two onward and shifts the primary activity from content review to question practice by month three. Students who spend all six months on content review reach test day without the timing stamina and section pacing that full-length practice exams develop. You cannot fake those skills in the last two weeks.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many hours per day should you study for the MCAT?

During dedicated preparation (the final 3 months before your exam), most successful MCAT students study 6 to 10 hours per day, 6 days per week. During the earlier 3 months of a 6-month plan, 3 to 5 hours daily is appropriate for content review. Total preparation hours vary widely among high scorers, but 400 to 500 hours over a 6-month period is a common range for students who reach a 515 or higher. Quality of study matters as much as quantity: unfocused review time is far less valuable than focused Qbank practice with thorough review.

What is the best MCAT prep resource?

The Jack Westin daily MCAT CARS passages are widely used for daily reading comprehension practice. Khan Academy MCAT (now legacy content) covers Biological and Biochemical Foundations thoroughly. Princeton Review's Complete MCAT book is a popular content review reference. For question practice, UWorld MCAT and the AAMC Official Qbank are the highest-quality resources and most accurately reflect the exam's style and difficulty. Most high scorers use official AAMC materials heavily in the final 4 to 6 weeks.

How do you structure a 6-month MCAT study plan?

Months 1 and 2 focus on content review of the highest-yield subjects: Biology, Biochemistry, and Psychology/Sociology. Months 3 and 4 continue content review (Chemistry, Physics, CARS) while introducing Qbank practice (20 to 40 questions per day with review). Months 5 and 6 are dedicated Qbank practice and full-length exams. Take a full-length practice exam every 2 weeks from month 3 onward, then every week in the final month. Never take a full-length exam without reviewing every wrong and uncertain answer.

When should you take a full-length MCAT practice exam?

Take your first official AAMC full-length practice exam about 3 months before your test date to establish a baseline. Take additional full-lengths every 2 to 3 weeks until the final month, then increase to weekly. Take your last full-length 7 to 10 days before your actual exam to avoid burnout and leave time to review. AAMC full-length practice exams are the most accurate predictors of your actual MCAT score. Third-party exams (Kaplan, Princeton Review) are generally harder and less predictive.

What is the hardest MCAT section?

CARS (Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills) is widely considered the hardest section to improve because it tests analytical reasoning rather than content knowledge. You cannot study your way to a higher CARS score the same way you can for science sections. Instead, daily practice with dense, humanities-style passages (philosophy, social science, literary criticism) builds the reading speed and argument-analysis skills the section requires. Most prep programs recommend 1 passage per day throughout your entire study period, not just in the final weeks.
