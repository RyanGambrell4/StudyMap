Who We Are

The StudyEdge AI editorial team is made up of people who have spent years in and around academic learning: researchers who study how memory works, instructors who have watched thousands of students struggle with ineffective habits, and coaches who have helped students turn their grades around. We are not a content mill. We write about study science because we care about it.

Every article on this blog starts with a research question, not a keyword. We identify what students actually get wrong about a given topic, find the strongest available evidence on what works, and then write in plain language that a first-year college student can act on immediately. If we cannot find solid research to support a claim, we do not make it.

Our team draws on backgrounds in educational psychology, cognitive science, academic advising, and standardized test preparation. We cross-check each other's work, track the journals that matter most in learning science, and revisit articles when new findings warrant it. The goal is a blog that functions as a reliable reference, not just a collection of generic study tips.

When we cite a study, we link directly to the original paper so you can read it yourself. When findings are contested or context-dependent, we say so rather than presenting oversimplified takeaways. That level of honesty is what distinguishes evidence-based content from the kind of recycled advice that fills most study tip articles.

Editorial Standards

  • Peer-reviewed sources only. Every factual claim is supported by research published in peer-reviewed academic journals. We do not cite self-help books, opinion pieces, or studies that have not survived editorial review.
  • Claims are verified independently. When a study is cited, a team member reads the original paper, not a secondary summary. Effect sizes, sample sizes, and methodology are checked before any claim is published.
  • Contested findings are labeled. Some areas of learning science have mixed or evolving evidence. When that is the case, we say so explicitly rather than presenting false certainty.
  • Practical applicability is required. Research gets included only when it has clear implications for what a college student should actually do. Interesting but non-actionable findings are excluded.
  • Articles are reviewed on a rolling basis. We revisit high-traffic articles at least once a year, or sooner when a significant new study is published. The date shown on each article reflects the most recent substantive revision.
  • No conflicts of interest in research citations. We do not cite studies funded by companies whose products we recommend without disclosing that relationship. StudyEdge AI is mentioned in articles because it is relevant to the topic, never as a condition of covering the research.
