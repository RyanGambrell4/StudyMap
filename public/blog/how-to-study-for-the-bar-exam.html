How to Study for the Bar Exam: A 10-Week Study Plan

Published July 19, 2026 Updated Jul 2026 · 12 min read

Share: Share on X
Key Takeaways
  • The first-time UBE pass rate is approximately 60% nationally (NCBE, 2023) -- most failures are preparation failures, not intelligence failures
  • Plan for 400 to 500 total study hours across 10 weeks -- roughly 8 to 10 focused hours per day, 5 to 6 days per week
  • Active recall and practice questions beat passive outline reading -- Roediger and Karpicke (2006) found retrieval practice produced 50% better retention than re-reading alone
  • MBE subjects (Contracts, Torts, Con Law, Criminal Law and Procedure, Evidence, Real Property, Civil Procedure) require the most attention -- they drive both the multiple-choice and essay scores
  • Timed essay and MPT practice must start by Week 3, not the week before the exam
  • The most common failure mode is spending too many hours passively re-reading outlines instead of doing practice problems and writing timed essays

The bar exam is one of the longest, most demanding credentialing exams in the United States. In 2023, the National Conference of Bar Examiners (NCBE) reported a first-time UBE pass rate of approximately 60% nationwide. That number is sobering, but it also contains a clarifying message: the majority of failures are not failures of legal aptitude. They are failures of preparation method and preparation volume.

This guide covers what the bar exam actually tests, how to structure a realistic 10-week preparation plan, why active recall produces better results than passive outline reading, and the specific mistakes that reliably produce failing scores even among candidates who feel prepared.

Bottom line up front: You need 400 to 500 total study hours, you need to start practicing questions on each subject within a day or two of learning the outline, and you need to write timed essays starting no later than Week 3. Passive re-reading of outlines is the most common preparation mistake and the one most likely to produce a failing score.

Understanding the Bar Exam Structure

If you are sitting for the Uniform Bar Exam (UBE), adopted by the majority of U.S. jurisdictions, the exam consists of three components spread across two days.

Day 1: The Multistate Essay Examination (MEE) -- six 30-minute essays -- followed by two 90-minute Multistate Performance Tests (MPT). The MEE tests your ability to identify and analyze legal issues in written form. The MPT tests your ability to complete a realistic legal task (a memo, a brief, a client letter) using a set of provided source documents, without outside knowledge.

Day 2: The Multistate Bar Examination (MBE) -- 200 multiple-choice questions split into two 100-question sessions. The MBE is weighted heavily: in UBE jurisdictions, the MBE accounts for 50% of your total score.

Seven subjects appear on the MBE: Contracts (including UCC Article 2), Torts, Constitutional Law, Criminal Law and Procedure, Evidence, Real Property, and Civil Procedure. These same subjects also appear frequently in MEE essays, which means investing heavily in MBE subjects has a dual return on your score.

MEE-only subjects -- tested on the essay component but not the MBE -- include Family Law, Secured Transactions, Business Associations (Corporations and Agency/Partnership), Conflicts of Law, and Trusts and Estates. These require coverage but a lighter study allocation than MBE subjects.

State-specific bar exams: If your state does not use the UBE, the structure differs. California, for example, has its own essay component and a different weight distribution. Check your state's bar exam specifications directly from the Board of Bar Examiners website before finalizing your study plan.

How Many Hours You Actually Need

The consensus among bar prep programs, bar exam coaches, and published pass-rate analyses is that successful first-time candidates study between 400 and 500 total hours across their preparation period. At 10 weeks, that is 40 to 50 hours per week -- roughly 8 to 10 hours per day for 5 days per week, with one or two shorter days for recovery.

That number shocks most candidates. Law school finals were survivable on far fewer hours. The difference is scope. The bar exam does not test one semester of material; it tests seven distinct subjects at a depth sufficient for professional licensing. The hour requirement is structural, not aspirational.

The 400-hour floor is real

Students who study fewer than 300 hours fail at disproportionately high rates regardless of their law school GPA. The bar exam does not reward intelligence that is not applied to practice questions and timed writing. It rewards preparation volume combined with the right methods.

If you are working full-time during bar prep, a 10-week timeline almost certainly does not work. A realistic working-candidate window is 14 to 16 weeks at 25 to 30 hours per week. The total hour target does not change; the timeline does.

The 10-Week Bar Exam Study Plan

The following schedule is structured for a full-time bar preparer starting 10 weeks before the exam. Adjust subject order if your jurisdiction has specific areas of emphasis, but do not defer MBE subjects past Week 6.

Week Primary Focus Daily Hours Key Activities
Week 1 Contracts + Torts 8 hrs Outline review; 30 MBE Qs/subject/day; start retrieval flashcards
Week 2 Con Law + Criminal Law & Procedure 8 hrs Outline + immediate MBE drilling; review Week 1 material via active recall
Week 3 Evidence + Real Property 9 hrs Outline + MBE drilling; first 2 timed MEE essays; MPT orientation
Week 4 Civil Procedure + MEE subjects (Family Law, Secured Transactions) 9 hrs Outline + drilling; 2 timed MEE essays; first full MPT simulation
Week 5 MEE subjects (Business Associations, Trusts & Estates, Conflicts) 9 hrs Outline + MEE essays for each subject; MBE cross-subject review
Week 6 MBE intensive: mixed subject drilling 10 hrs 50-question timed MBE sets; essay writing (2/day); second MPT simulation
Week 7 Weak subjects identified from practice scores 10 hrs Targeted drilling on lowest-scoring MBE subjects; essay feedback review
Week 8 Full-length MBE timed blocks + essay writing 10 hrs 100-question timed MBE sets; 3 essays/day; third MPT simulation
Week 9 First full simulated bar exam day 10 hrs Complete Day 1 simulation (MEE + MPT); complete Day 2 simulation (MBE)
Week 10 Targeted review + final simulation 8 hrs (taper) Second full simulation; review wrong answers; sleep and recovery priority

How to Study MBE Subjects

The MBE is a 200-question multiple-choice exam, and it accounts for half of your UBE score. Most candidates underperform on the MBE not because they do not know the rules, but because they know the rules in the abstract and cannot apply them quickly under time pressure to a fact pattern they have never seen.

The solution is not more outline reading. It is more practice questions, done correctly.

The right way to practice MBE questions

For each MBE subject, the process should go as follows. Read the outline for the subject -- spend one day, two at most. Then immediately begin doing 25 to 30 practice questions on that subject. Do not wait until you feel ready. The questions will reveal exactly which rules you do not actually understand, which is far more useful than another passive review of the outline.

After each set of questions, review every wrong answer. Do not just note which answer was correct. Write out, in your own words, the rule that produces the correct answer. This is retrieval practice applied to legal rules, and In 2006, Roediger and Karpicke demonstrated that writing out retrieved information from memory produces dramatically better retention than recognition-based review. Writing the rule out forces you to construct the memory, not just confirm it.

Target 2,000 to 2,500 practice MBE questions across your full preparation period. Track your accuracy by subject from the beginning. Your lowest-scoring subjects should receive the most practice time in Weeks 7 and 8.

How to distribute your MBE subject time

Allocate MBE study time roughly proportional to the question distribution. Contracts, Torts, and Evidence each have approximately 25 to 27 questions on the actual exam. Constitutional Law, Criminal Law and Procedure, Real Property, and Civil Procedure each have approximately 25. No subject should receive fewer than 200 practice questions across your preparation period.

Spaced repetition applies to MBE subjects just as it does to any academic subject. In 2013, Dunlosky and colleagues confirmed that distributed practice -- returning to the same material across multiple sessions separated by days -- produces substantially better retention than massed practice sessions. Do not spend three consecutive days on Contracts and then not touch it for two weeks. Practice each subject every few days throughout the preparation period, even in small doses.

How to Study for MEE Essays

The Multistate Essay Examination consists of six essays, each answered in 30 minutes. The essays test issue spotting, rule identification, and organized written analysis. They do not require you to memorize entire treatises; they require you to identify the relevant legal framework and apply it to the given facts in a structured way.

The single most important thing you can do to improve your MEE scores is to write timed practice essays and compare them against published NCBE model answers. This is not comfortable. Most candidates prefer to read through model answers without writing their own response first, because reading feels productive and writing a bad essay feels discouraging. But reading model answers without writing your own first is passive review -- it builds recognition, not production ability. On exam day, you need to produce a structured legal analysis from a blank page under time pressure, not recognize a good answer when you see one.

The essay review process

The most effective essay review process is: set a 30-minute timer, write your complete answer, stop when time is up, then compare your answer line-by-line to the model answer. Mark specifically which issues you missed entirely, which rules you stated incorrectly, and which application paragraphs were incomplete. Each gap becomes an item for your active recall review deck.

Write at least two timed MEE essays per week starting in Week 3. By Week 8, you should be writing three to four essays per day. The volume sounds excessive until you calculate that the actual exam requires three essays in a morning session. You need to have written enough essays under time pressure that the format is automatic.

Use focused study sessions for essay writing -- no phone, no interruptions, a real timer running. Essay writing under genuine time pressure is a skill that only develops through practice under genuine time pressure.

How to Approach the MPT

The Multistate Performance Test is two 90-minute tasks. You receive a file of source documents -- cases, statutes, a fact memo -- and are asked to produce a legal work product such as a persuasive brief, an objective memo, or a letter to a client. You are not expected to bring outside legal knowledge; all the law you need is in the provided materials.

Most candidates spend too little time practicing the MPT before the exam and then run out of time during it. The MPT is not primarily a knowledge test; it is a time management and document-synthesis test. Candidates who score poorly on the MPT typically either spend too long reading the source documents before starting to write, or try to write a comprehensive answer when a complete-but-leaner one would score higher.

Complete at least four full MPT simulations at 90 minutes each before the exam. The goal is not to write a perfect answer; it is to develop a consistent approach to the task format so that on exam day, you spend zero cognitive energy figuring out what to do first.

Why Active Recall Beats Re-Reading for Bar Prep

Most bar prep programs provide extensive outlines. Some candidates spend the majority of their preparation time reading and re-reading those outlines, highlighting rules, and writing condensed notes. This approach is comfortable, measurable by page count, and largely ineffective for long-term retention.

In 2006, Roediger and Karpicke published a study in Psychological Science that has become foundational to the science of learning. They divided students into two groups: one group re-read a passage multiple times, and the other read it once and then practiced retrieving the content from memory. A week later, the retrieval group retained 50% more information than the re-reading group. The re-reading group's advantage at immediate recall disappeared almost entirely over time.

The mechanism is not complicated. Every time you successfully retrieve a piece of information from memory, the neural pathway storing that information is strengthened. The act of retrieval -- not the act of exposure -- is what builds durable memory. Passive re-reading produces familiarity. Familiarity tells you nothing about whether you can reproduce the rule under exam conditions.

For bar prep, this translates directly: spend less time reading outlines and more time doing practice questions and writing timed essays without consulting the outline first. The discomfort of not knowing the answer is not wasted time. It is the learning mechanism working.

In 2013, Dunlosky and colleagues reviewed 10 study techniques and rated practice testing and distributed practice as the only two methods with strong enough evidence to be called high utility. Both are directly applicable to bar prep. Practice testing is what you do with MBE questions and timed essays. Distributed practice is what you do when you spread your study sessions for each subject across weeks rather than massing them into a single block.

Common Bar Exam Preparation Mistakes

The following mistakes account for a disproportionate share of failing scores. None of them are mistakes of intelligence. They are mistakes of preparation method and preparation structure.

Starting too late

Ten weeks is the minimum viable window for a full-time preparer aiming for 400 hours. Starting 8 weeks out means either studying 12-hour days or arriving at the exam with 250 to 300 hours -- a range that correlates with significantly higher failure rates. If your exam is in two months and you have not started, start immediately and consider whether requesting a later administration is strategically wiser than racing through compressed preparation.

Spending too much time on passive outline reading

Outlines are a starting point, not a study method. Reading the Contracts outline for the fourth time is almost zero-value study time. Every hour spent re-reading outlines after the first pass would produce better results if redirected to practice questions. Use the outline to look up rules you got wrong on practice questions. Do not use it as the primary vehicle of bar prep.

Not doing enough practice MBE questions

The most reliable predictor of MBE performance is the volume of quality practice questions completed before the exam. Candidates who complete 1,500 or fewer practice questions arrive at the exam without the procedural fluency needed to process 200 questions at 1.8 minutes each. Target 2,000 to 2,500 questions. Track your accuracy by subject from the beginning so you can identify and address weak areas before Week 8.

Not timing essay practice

Writing a MEE essay in 45 minutes with the model answer open in front of you is not essay practice. It is model-answer reading with extra steps. The skill the exam requires is producing a complete, issue-spotted, rule-stated, applied analysis in 30 minutes from a cold start. That skill only develops through repeated cold-start, timed essay writing. Set the timer. Write the full answer. Then review.

Neglecting sleep in the final two weeks

In 2011, Diekelmann published research confirming that sleep plays a central role in memory consolidation: the transfer of newly learned information into stable long-term storage happens during slow-wave and REM sleep. An all-night cramming session the night before the bar exam does not help. It eliminates the consolidation window for everything you have worked to learn. In the final two weeks, protect your sleep schedule as aggressively as you protect your study schedule.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many hours should I study for the bar exam?

Most bar prep experts and commercial bar prep programs recommend 400 to 500 total study hours across a 10-week preparation period. That works out to roughly 8 to 10 focused hours per day, 5 to 6 days per week. The NCBE has consistently reported that first-time UBE takers who pass cluster significantly higher in preparation intensity than those who fail. Part-time working candidates who cannot reach 400 hours typically need a longer preparation window -- 14 to 16 weeks -- rather than trying to compress the same material into fewer daily hours. Quality matters too: 8 hours of active practice-question-based study is more valuable than 8 hours of passive outline reading.

What bar subjects should I study first?

Prioritize MBE subjects first because they appear on both the multiple-choice section and are heavily tested across MEE essays. The seven MBE subjects are Contracts, Torts, Constitutional Law, Criminal Law and Procedure, Evidence, Real Property, and Civil Procedure. Start with whichever subject you are weakest in -- there is no fixed universal starting order -- but ensure all seven receive dedicated coverage in the first six weeks. MEE-only subjects such as Family Law, Secured Transactions, Business Associations, Conflicts of Law, and Trusts and Estates should be covered in Weeks 5 through 8 after MBE subjects are established.

Is it possible to pass the bar exam in 8 weeks?

An 8-week preparation is possible but leaves very little margin for error. The core risk is that an 8-week window forces you to compress your MBE subject coverage, which leaves insufficient time for spaced repetition of the material. Research on memory consolidation (Diekelmann, 2011) shows that distributed review sessions over time produce substantially better retention than massed study. If you have only 8 weeks, prioritize MBE practice questions over outline review, skip passive re-reading entirely, and begin full timed essay practice starting in Week 3 rather than Week 5. If you have any flexibility, 10 weeks is a materially better window.

What is the best bar prep course?

The most widely used commercial bar prep courses are Barbri and Themis. Both provide structured outlines, MBE question banks, and lecture series. The best course is the one you will actually complete consistently. The critical variable is not which commercial program you use but how actively you engage with practice questions versus how much time you spend passively watching lectures or re-reading outlines. Dunlosky et al. (2013) rated practice testing as the single highest-utility study technique available. Use the commercial course for structure and outlines, but never let it become a vehicle for passive review.

How should I practice MBE questions?

The most effective MBE practice method is to do questions in sets of 17 to 34, review every wrong answer immediately, and write out the rule that produces the correct answer in your own words. Do not skip question review -- that is where learning happens. Aim for 2,000 to 2,500 practice MBE questions across your full preparation period. In the final three weeks, move to full 100-question timed blocks to build the stamina and time management the actual exam requires. Track your accuracy by subject to identify which areas need concentrated work in the final weeks.
