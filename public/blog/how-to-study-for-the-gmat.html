How to Study for GMAT

The GMAT is the longest-standing MBA admissions test. The current GMAT Focus Edition has three sections: Quantitative Reasoning, Verbal Reasoning, and Data Insights. Here is how to plan a 3- to 6-month prep cycle and which patterns actually move your score.

Key Takeaways
  • GMAT Quant tests logical reasoning more than advanced math -- most content is at 8th-10th grade math level applied to tricky setups
  • Verbal (Critical Reasoning and Reading Comprehension) rewards structured reasoning -- identify premise, conclusion, and assumption before answering
  • Integrated Reasoning is machine-scored and often neglected; a strong IR score signals analytical ability that business schools value
  • The official GMAC prep materials (OG, OG Verbal, OG Quant) are the most accurate indicators of actual exam questions -- prioritize these over third-party books
  • Accuracy on harder questions matters more than finishing all questions; aggressive time management on easy questions frees more time for harder ones

The new GMAT Focus structure

GMAT Focus removed AWA, sentence correction (mostly), and Integrated Reasoning is now part of Data Insights. Total time is 2 hours 15 minutes. You can review and skip questions within sections. Plan around the new format, not the old.

The average GMAT test-taker studies between 150 and 200 hours before reaching their target score. Students who reach 200 hours without meaningful score improvement have usually been doing the same kind of practice repeatedly rather than identifying and fixing specific error patterns that are holding the score down.

Quant: harder than the GRE, simpler than the SAT

GMAT quant rewards problem framing more than calculation. Data Sufficiency is the section that decides scores in the 700-plus range. Most takers underprepare for it because it is unlike anything from their undergraduate work. Drill DS in 30-question blocks.

Verbal: critical reasoning and reading comp

Critical Reasoning is essentially LSAT logical reasoning with a business twist. Many of the strategies transfer. Reading Comprehension uses dense passages where extracting structure beats reading every word.

Data Insights, the new heavyweight

Data Insights blends graph interpretation, multi-source reasoning, table analysis, and two-part analysis. Most prep books still focus on the legacy IR section; the format has changed. Use official GMAC practice for the closest simulation.

Use official practice exhaustively

Official Guide problems and GMAC's Focus practice tests are the only source where the difficulty and style match the real test. Magoosh, Manhattan, and Veritas are good for explanations and drill volume; OG is for realism.

Build a study plan around your test date

12-week minimum for most takers, 24 weeks for a major score jump (550 to 700+). A study schedule generator can lay out the week-by-week structure automatically from your test date. Within the plan: weeks 1 to 4 on content review and easy drilling, weeks 5 to 8 on focused weakness work, weeks 9 to 11 on timed sections and full tests, week 12 on test prep and rest.

Score plateaus

Most takers plateau in the high 600s. Breaking 700 requires diagnosing which question subtypes you keep missing. Pull every wrong answer from your last 3 tests, tag by subtype, and grind the worst three subtypes for two weeks.

GMAT score improvement is not about doing more practice. It is about doing targeted practice on the specific question types and reasoning gaps where you are currently losing points.

Test day

Sleep matters. Avoid new techniques late in prep. Take your last full test 4 to 7 days before. Light review only in the final 2 days. Eat the same breakfast you ate before your best practice tests. Trust the prep.

The bottom line on GMAT prep

GMAT performance is about practice quality more than practice volume. Students who take practice tests without reviewing every wrong answer in detail are wasting the most valuable part of the process. The wrong answers reveal the specific reasoning gaps and timing patterns that are holding the score below target. Identifying those patterns and fixing them specifically -- rather than doing more practice of the same kind -- is what produces score improvement in the 700-plus range.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to prepare for the GMAT?

Most test-takers need 3 to 6 months of dedicated preparation to see significant score improvements. Students starting below their target by 50 or more points typically need the longer end of that range. The GMAT rewards practice volume and pattern recognition: your score improves as you recognize question types and eliminate wrong answers faster. Weekly full-length practice tests combined with focused section review produces more improvement than content review alone.

What is a good GMAT score for business school?

The average GMAT score at the top 10 MBA programs is between 720 and 740. A score above 700 is generally competitive for top-25 programs. Median scores vary significantly by program, and the GMAT is evaluated alongside GPA, work experience, and essays. Check the reported median score for each school you are targeting and aim to be at or above the median for your application to be considered competitive on the quantitative dimension.

How is the GMAT structured?

The GMAT consists of four sections: Analytical Writing Assessment (30 minutes), Integrated Reasoning (30 minutes, 12 questions), Quantitative Reasoning (62 minutes, 31 questions), and Verbal Reasoning (65 minutes, 36 questions). Scores range from 200 to 800 on a combined scale. The exam is computer adaptive: question difficulty adjusts based on your performance. Answering early questions correctly is disproportionately important because they set the difficulty band for subsequent questions.

Is the GMAT or GRE better for MBA applications?

Most MBA programs now accept both, and neither is consistently preferred. The GMAT has historically been associated with business school and may still carry more weight at some programs. The GRE is accepted at virtually all MBA programs and may be advantageous for students applying to both business and other graduate programs simultaneously. Take a free practice test for each and compare your starting scores before committing to one.

What GMAT prep resources are most effective?

Official GMAT practice exams from MBA.com use the actual adaptive algorithm and are the closest simulation to test day. The Official Guide to the GMAT provides authentic retired questions. For Quant, Manhattan Prep's strategy guides are highly regarded. For Verbal, especially Critical Reasoning, studying the structure of arguments rather than using intuition significantly improves accuracy. Tracking your error types across practice sessions helps focus your remaining preparation time.
