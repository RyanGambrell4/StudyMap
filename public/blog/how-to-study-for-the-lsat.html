How to Study for LSAT

The LSAT is a logic test wearing a law test costume. Most takers walk in expecting reading comprehension and constitutional knowledge and meet a section on formal logic instead. Here is how to study for the LSAT in a way that scales: 3-month plan, 6-month plan, the sections that decide your score, and the patterns to drill.

Why the LSAT punishes content review

The LSAT does not test what you know. It tests how fast and accurately you reason about new material. Reading more about constitutional law does not raise your score. Doing more logic games under time does. Treat the test like a skill test, not a knowledge test.

The three sections and what they actually reward

Logical Reasoning rewards pattern recognition in argument structure. Logic Games (now removed from the LSAT but historically the score-mover, replaced by an additional LR section) rewarded diagramming speed. Reading Comprehension rewards extracting argument structure under time. All three reward the same underlying skill: structural analysis fast.

Build the foundation first, then drill

First 4 weeks: read through a reputable guide like the Loophole or PowerScore Bibles. Learn the question types. Do untimed problems. Build the structural vocabulary. Then switch to timed practice; without the vocabulary, timed practice teaches you to make the same mistakes faster.

Drill by question type, not by random section

Pull all the necessary assumption questions across 20 PrepTests. Do them in a row. Then do all the strengthen questions. The category drilling builds pattern recognition. Mixed timed sets come later.

Take a full timed practice test weekly

Once you have built the foundation, simulate the test. Real timing. Real conditions. Score it. Review every wrong answer thoroughly: not just why the right answer is right, but why you picked the wrong one. The pattern in your wrong answers is the next thing to drill.

Score plateaus and how to break them

Most takers plateau at 158 to 162 because they have built basic skill but have not drilled the harder question subtypes. Breaking 165 requires identifying which specific question types are dragging you down (parallel reasoning, principle, role-of-statement) and grinding them in focused sessions, not adding more general practice.

Timing strategies that actually work

Skip and return on Logical Reasoning. Build the right answer in your head before checking the choices. Do not eliminate; instead predict. On reading comprehension, skim for structure first (claim, support, exception) before reading top to bottom.

How long should you actually study

Most successful takers report 250 to 400 hours total over 3 to 6 months. Shorter than that is hard to fit the necessary drilling. Longer than that hits diminishing returns. Budget about 15 to 20 hours per week if you are doing a 4 to 5 month plan. A study schedule generator can help you map that timeline week by week.

The test day plan

Sleep matters. Do not learn new techniques in the final week. Take a half-length test 2 days out. Take the day before fully off. Walk in with one cup of coffee, a tested snack at the break, and the same routine you used in practice.
