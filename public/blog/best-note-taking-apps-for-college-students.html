1. Notion, Best All-in-One Workspace

Notion

All-in-one workspace: notes, databases, tasks, and wikis

Free personal plan / $10/mo Plus

Notion has become the dominant note-taking platform for college students who want more than just notes, it's a full workspace with databases, kanban boards, linked pages, and a growing AI layer. Students use it to manage everything from course notes to project trackers to personal wikis.

Pros

  • Free for personal use with nearly all features
  • Highly flexible: pages, databases, calendars, kanban
  • Thousands of student templates available
  • Good for group projects and shared notebooks
  • Works on all platforms (web, iOS, Android, desktop)

Cons

  • Can feel overwhelming until you build your system
  • AI features cost $10/mo as an add-on
  • Slower than simpler apps on older hardware
  • Not ideal for handwriting on iPad

Notion works especially well for students in business, social sciences, and humanities who want structured notebooks with relational databases linking courses, assignments, and notes. Pre-med and STEM students often prefer simpler apps focused purely on content rather than organization overhead.

Notion verdict

Best for: students who want an all-in-one system for notes, tasks, and planning. Free for personal use. Steeper learning curve than simpler apps, but the flexibility pays off for detail-oriented students.

2. Obsidian, Best for Connected Knowledge

Obsidian

Local-first, bidirectional linking for a personal knowledge base

Free (personal) / $8/mo Sync

Obsidian takes a completely different philosophy from Notion: every note is a local markdown file, and the app's superpower is bidirectional linking, creating a web of connected ideas that mirrors how knowledge actually builds in your mind. It's become particularly popular with pre-law, philosophy, and research-focused students.

Pros

  • Completely free for local personal use
  • Notes stored as markdown files you own forever
  • Powerful backlinks create a visual knowledge graph
  • Active plugin ecosystem (Spaced Repetition, Dataview, etc.)
  • Privacy-first: no cloud required

Cons

  • Sync across devices costs $8/mo (or use iCloud/Git)
  • Higher setup investment than Notion or OneNote
  • No native collaboration features
  • Not ideal for visual or handwritten notes

Obsidian verdict

Best for: research-heavy students who think in interconnected ideas, pre-law, philosophy, history, computer science. Powerful and free, but requires more setup than other apps on this list.

3. Goodnotes 6, Best for iPad Handwriting

Goodnotes 6

Digital handwriting and PDF annotation for iPad + Apple Pencil

$17.99 one-time purchase

If you have an iPad and Apple Pencil, Goodnotes is the leading app for handwritten notes. Research suggests handwriting notes improves comprehension and retention compared to typing (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014), Goodnotes delivers a best-in-class writing experience with excellent handwriting recognition, PDF annotation, and organization.

Pros

  • Best handwriting experience on iPad, natural feel
  • Excellent PDF annotation for textbooks and papers
  • Handwriting OCR makes notes searchable
  • One-time purchase ($17.99), no subscription
  • Great for math, chemistry, and diagram-heavy courses

Cons

  • Apple only, no Android or Windows support
  • Requires iPad + Apple Pencil for best experience
  • No AI flashcard generation or study features
  • Not ideal for long-form typed notes

Goodnotes verdict

Best for: iPad users who take handwritten notes in math, science, or diagram-heavy courses. One-time purchase makes it affordable. Only relevant if you have an iPad + Apple Pencil.

4. OneNote, Best Free Option for Windows/Microsoft Users

Microsoft OneNote

Free, flexible notebook for the Microsoft ecosystem

Completely free with Microsoft account

OneNote is Microsoft's free note-taking app, available on every platform. For students with a university Microsoft 365 account (most US universities provide this), OneNote integrates seamlessly with Word, Teams, and OneDrive. It's not the most exciting app, but it's reliable, free, and works everywhere.

Pros

  • Completely free with any Microsoft account
  • Works on Windows, Mac, iOS, Android, and web
  • Good for mixing text, images, drawings, and audio
  • Real-time collaboration with teammates
  • Integrates with Microsoft 365 suite

Cons

  • Less modern interface than Notion or Obsidian
  • Limited markdown support
  • AI features require Microsoft 365 Copilot subscription
  • Syncing can be slow on mobile

OneNote verdict

Best for: students already in the Microsoft ecosystem who want a free, no-friction note-taking app. Not as feature-rich as Notion, but completely free and works on every platform.

5. Notability, Best for Handwriting + Audio

Notability

Handwritten notes with synchronized audio recording

$14.99/year

Notability's killer feature is synchronized audio recording: tap any word in your notes and it jumps to that exact moment in the lecture recording. For students who benefit from reviewing lectures rather than just their notes, this is genuinely unique. The handwriting experience is excellent, though most Goodnotes users will find both comparable.

Pros

  • Synchronized audio recording is unique and powerful
  • Excellent handwriting experience on iPad
  • AI note summaries and organization features
  • Flash cards can be created from notes

Cons

  • Apple only, iPad/iPhone/Mac
  • Subscription ($14.99/yr) vs. Goodnotes one-time purchase
  • Study tools are more basic than dedicated study apps

Notability verdict

Best for: students who record lectures and want to review them in sync with their notes. Strong choice for lecture-heavy courses in law, humanities, and social sciences.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best note-taking app for college students?

The best note-taking app depends on your workflow. Notion is best for students who want an all-in-one workspace with databases and templates. Obsidian is best for students who think in connected ideas (popular in pre-law and research). Goodnotes is best for iPad users who take handwritten notes. OneNote is best for students already in the Microsoft ecosystem. For students who want AI to automatically convert notes into flashcards and practice exams, StudyEdge AI is designed specifically for that workflow.

Is Notion or Obsidian better for college students?

Notion is better for students who want structured databases, task management, and collaboration, especially popular for group projects and course planning. Obsidian is better for students building a personal knowledge base with deep bidirectional linking, particularly popular with pre-law, philosophy, and research-heavy students. Obsidian stores files locally (privacy-friendly); Notion is cloud-only.

Is Goodnotes worth it for college students?

Goodnotes is worth it for college students with an iPad and Apple Pencil who prefer handwriting notes. Research suggests handwriting improves comprehension compared to typing, and Goodnotes offers excellent handwriting recognition, PDF annotation, and organization. The one-time $17.99 purchase makes it affordable. If you don't have an iPad, most other apps on this list are a better fit for laptop use.

What is the best free note-taking app for college students?

The best free note-taking apps for college students are: Notion (free for personal use with most features), OneNote (completely free with a Microsoft account), Obsidian (free for personal use with local storage), and Apple Notes (free on Apple devices). All four cover the core note-taking workflow without payment. StudyEdge AI also has a free plan that adds AI flashcard generation and practice exam creation on top of note storage.

How do I choose a note-taking app for college?

Choose based on three factors: your devices (iPad vs. laptop vs. both), your note style (typed vs. handwritten vs. visual), and your study workflow (notes only vs. notes + study tools). If you have an iPad and like handwriting: Goodnotes or Notability. If you type notes and want organization: Notion or Obsidian. If you want your notes to automatically become study materials (flashcards, practice exams): StudyEdge AI. If you want free and simple: OneNote.
