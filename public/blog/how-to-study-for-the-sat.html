How to Study for SAT

The Digital SAT runs 2 hours 14 minutes, is adaptive at the section level, and covers Reading and Writing plus Math. Here is how to prep over 8 to 16 weeks depending on starting score and target.

Key Takeaways
  • SAT scores are highly coachable -- the exam tests a defined set of skills, not general intelligence, and those skills are trainable
  • Reading passages test your ability to find evidence for claims, not comprehend content -- approach each question as an evidence-location task
  • Grammar and writing questions follow a small set of testable rules (subject-verb agreement, parallelism, punctuation) -- learning these rules eliminates most wrong answers
  • Math without a calculator tests algebra and data interpretation, not advanced math -- speed and accuracy on basic algebraic manipulation is the bottleneck
  • Official College Board practice tests are the most accurate prep material; third-party tests vary significantly in difficulty calibration

What the Digital SAT actually tests

Reading and Writing is shorter passages with comprehension and grammar mixed together. Math is on-screen with a built-in Desmos calculator for every problem. The format rewards speed and pattern recognition over deep content; you have about 70 seconds per question.

The average SAT score is approximately 1020 out of 1600. The gap between a 1200 and a 1400 is roughly 20 percentile points and is driven primarily by reading comprehension accuracy and math fluency -- not by test-taking strategy, vocabulary memorization, or time spent on non-official prep materials.

Diagnose first

Take an official Bluebook practice test before any prep. The College Board's Bluebook app has 6 full-length digital SATs. Whichever score range you start in determines the realistic target and the prep length.

Reading and Writing strategy

Most questions are answerable from a short passage. Read the question first, then the passage with the question in mind. Vocab-in-context questions reward predicting the meaning before reading the choices.

Math: master the calculator

Desmos is built in. Learn to graph equations, find intersections, and use it for substitution checks. The students who never open Desmos leave easy points on the table.

Targeted drilling by subtopic

Pull 20 algebra problems, do them in a row. Then 20 geometry. Then 20 data analysis. Subtopic drilling builds the pattern recognition that mixed practice does not. A study schedule generator can map out your entire SAT prep timeline from today to test day automatically.

Vocabulary: smaller than you think

The Digital SAT cut explicit vocabulary questions. You still need a strong vocabulary for vocab-in-context items and for reading at speed. 200 to 400 high-frequency words is enough; do not over-invest.

Plan length by goal

Going from 1100 to 1300 in 8 weeks is realistic. 1100 to 1500 in 16 weeks is realistic with consistent work. 1100 to 1500 in 8 weeks is rare without intensive daily work.

Take full practice tests under realism

Once a week minimum in the last month. Full timing. Same time of day as your real test. Real morning, real food, real environment. The first time you simulate test conditions should not be test day.

Official College Board SAT practice materials are the most accurate representation of what the exam actually tests. Students who drill primarily with unofficial practice tests are sometimes preparing for a test that differs enough from the real SAT to produce misleading score estimates.

Test day

Sleep 8 hours the night before, not just the week before. Walk in with the same routine you used in your best practice tests. Trust the prep; this is not the time to learn new techniques.

The bottom line on SAT prep

SAT scores are driven primarily by reading comprehension speed and accuracy, and by math fluency -- not by vocabulary memorization or test-taking tricks. The gap between a 1200 and a 1400 is almost always in passage reading accuracy and math problem-solving speed. Students who drill with official College Board materials, focus review on their highest-frequency error types, and build pacing discipline with timed sections see the most consistent score improvement over a structured prep period.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should you study for the SAT?

Most students benefit from 3 to 5 months of preparation, with 5 to 10 hours of study per week. Students who are far below their target score need the longer end of that range. The digital SAT (introduced in 2024) is computer-adaptive, shorter than the previous paper format, and has a modified Reading and Writing structure. Preparation should use official SAT practice tests from College Board and Khan Academy, which provides free personalized practice calibrated to your diagnostic results.

What is a good SAT score?

The average SAT score is approximately 1010 out of 1600. A score of 1100 to 1200 is above average for most four-year universities. Selective schools typically expect 1300 or higher, and highly selective schools expect 1450 to 1600. As with the ACT, your target should be at or above the 50th percentile of admitted students at the specific schools you are targeting, not a generic benchmark.

How is the digital SAT different from the old SAT?

The digital SAT is shorter (approximately 2 hours versus 3 hours), computer-adaptive by section (the second module in each section is harder or easier based on performance in the first), and has fewer questions per Reading passage. The Math section allows a calculator throughout. The Reading and Writing section combines reading comprehension and grammar in shorter, more focused passages. Preparation materials for the old paper SAT (pre-2024) are not ideal preparation for the current format.

How do you improve your SAT Math score?

SAT Math tests algebra, advanced math, problem-solving and data analysis, and geometry. The most effective preparation is working through official SAT practice problems categorized by topic and identifying which areas produce the most errors. The calculator is available for the entire Math section, so time management is less about calculation speed and more about setup accuracy. Students who consistently get problems wrong because of setup errors (not calculation errors) benefit most from slowing down and diagramming each problem before solving.

Is the SAT or ACT better for college admissions?

All major colleges accept both equally. The digital SAT places more emphasis on grammar and editing in the Reading and Writing section, while the ACT has a Science section that the SAT lacks. Students who are stronger at science reasoning and prefer more straightforward math sometimes prefer the ACT. Take a practice test for each and compare your starting scores, then prepare for whichever produces results closer to your target.
