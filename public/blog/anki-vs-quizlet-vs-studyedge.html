Anki: The Spaced Repetition Standard

Anki has been the tool of choice for serious memorizers since 2006. It's built entirely around the SM-2 spaced repetition algorithm, a system that schedules each card for review at precisely the moment you're about to forget it. This makes Anki dramatically more efficient than studying at fixed intervals or cramming before an exam.

What Anki does well

  • SM-2 spaced repetition: The most scientifically grounded implementation available. Cards you know well are reviewed less often; hard cards appear more frequently.
  • Community decks: The Anki shared deck library includes thousands of community decks. For pre-med students, the Anking deck covers USMLE Step 1 comprehensively.
  • Customization: Nearly unlimited control over card types, note fields, templates, and scheduling parameters.
  • Price: Free on desktop and Android. One-time $24.99 on iOS.

Where Anki falls short

  • Manual card creation: Building a quality Anki deck takes significant time. You write every card yourself unless you can find a pre-made deck that matches your exact course.
  • Steep learning curve: Anki's interface is dated and add-ons require configuration. New users often give up before getting value from it.
  • No AI generation: Anki doesn't automatically create flashcards from uploaded notes or syllabi.
  • No study planner: Anki shows how many cards are due today but doesn't plan sessions around upcoming exam dates.

Anki verdict

Best for pre-med, medical, and pharmacy students who need to memorize thousands of facts over months and are willing to invest time in setup. Also excellent for language learning. Not the right fit if you want something running in minutes.

Quizlet: The Largest Deck Library

Quizlet launched in 2005 and became the go-to flashcard tool for millions of students based largely on its shared deck library. For any course that uses a common textbook, there's a good chance someone has already built a Quizlet set. That zero-setup advantage drove its dominance for over a decade.

What Quizlet does well

  • Pre-made sets: The largest student-contributed flashcard library online. For standard textbooks, you can find and start studying in minutes.
  • Multiple study modes: Learn, Match, Test, and Gravity keep review engaging.
  • Familiarity: Most college students already know Quizlet, the onboarding friction is nearly zero.

Where Quizlet falls short in 2026

  • Paywalled AI features: Quizlet has locked most AI functionality behind Quizlet Plus ($7.99/month). Features that were free before 2024 now require a subscription.
  • Weak spaced repetition: Quizlet's Learn mode includes some adaptive scheduling, but it's significantly less sophisticated than Anki's SM-2 algorithm.
  • No study planner: No mechanism to build a study schedule around upcoming exam dates.
  • Accuracy risk with shared decks: Community decks can contain errors, always verify against your actual course material.

Quizlet verdict

Best for students who need to start quickly with a standard textbook and want to browse existing decks. Less compelling now that AI features cost $7.99/month, alternatives offer more at lower cost or for free.

The Study Science

All three apps use active recall, retrieving information from memory rather than passively re-reading, as their core mechanism. Research consistently shows active recall produces 2–3× better retention than re-reading or highlighting (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006).

Where they diverge is spaced repetition: scheduling review sessions at increasing intervals to reinforce memory at the optimal moment.

  • Anki uses the SM-2 algorithm. Cards you rate as "hard" appear sooner; cards you know well are pushed back. Highly calibrated and evidence-based.
  • Quizlet includes some adaptive logic in Learn mode on the Plus plan, but the algorithm is less sophisticated than Anki's SM-2.
  • StudyEdge AI integrates spaced repetition scheduling into its study planner automatically. Less customizable than Anki, but requires no manual configuration.

Our Recommendation

The best app depends on what you're studying and how you work:

  • Use Anki if you're pre-med, in medical school, studying pharmacology, or learning a language. SM-2 and community medical decks are hard to beat.
  • Use Quizlet if your course uses a standard textbook and you want existing decks rather than building from scratch. Budget for Plus if you want AI features.
  • Use StudyEdge AI if you want AI to generate your study material from your own notes and syllabus, get adaptive practice exams, and have a study coach plan sessions around your exam schedule, without the deck-building overhead.

Many students use more than one. A common combination: Anki for core memorization-heavy courses, StudyEdge AI for courses where AI-generated practice exams and a study planner deliver more value than brute-force card repetition.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Anki better than Quizlet for college students?

Anki has a more powerful spaced repetition algorithm (SM-2) than Quizlet, making it better for long-term retention in courses like organic chemistry, anatomy, and foreign languages. However, Quizlet is easier to set up and has a larger shared deck library. For students building their own material who want the best memory science, Anki wins. For quick access to existing decks, Quizlet is more practical.

Which study app is best for pre-med students?

How does StudyEdge AI compare to Anki?
